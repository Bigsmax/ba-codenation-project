1.5 - Describing copyright and other constraints affecting websites.

In general there is not copyright laws protecting websites as such, but there are descriptive classifications of “work” enumerated in the UKs Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988. Classifications of work include, Text, Html, CSS, Logo’s, Company Name, and Software. Databases have the benefit from the protection of the sui generis database right. Items classified as “work” that have requisite originality are extended copyright protection by the UK law.


1.6 - Describing access issues that need taking into account - describe issues that people with disabilities may face when using webpages and how we can overcome them.

Websites that don't consider web accessibility for people with disabilities tend to miss out on a huge chunk of the market. Web accessibility means that websites, tools, and technologies are designed and developed so that people with disabilities can use them. The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (known as WCAG 2.1) are an internationally recognised set of recommendations for improving web accessibility for users with impairments, including auditory - cognitive – neurological – physical - speech, and visual impairments. Recommendations to consider include: Use alt tags that clearly identify images, create subtitles and transcripts; this is particularly the case if the site is hosting videos (Hosting sites such as YouTube have tools that allow users to add subtitles to their clips). It is also recommended to use periods in abbreviations to help readers comprehend abbreviated items, in addition writing out links, rather just adding a click here link (underlining links etc.). Utilizing color control and avoid pairing colours that would be problematic for colour-blind users. Give clickable items a wider range to navigate around, and keep website copy simple and include an accessibility guide.
